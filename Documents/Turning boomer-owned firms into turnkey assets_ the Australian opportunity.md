# **Turning boomer-owned firms into turnkey assets: the Australian opportunity**

**Australia faces a trillion-dollar succession crisis, and professional services firms sit at the epicentre.** More than 500,000 businesses are owned by Australians aged 60 or older, yet only 24% of SME owners have any succession plan. For professional services firms — accounting, legal, financial planning, consulting, and marketing agencies — the gap between "running a practice" and "owning a saleable business" represents the single biggest value destruction risk in the sector. ThinkSwift's proposed service fills a genuine, well-defined market gap: no Australian firm currently offers an integrated, AI-native, operational-systemisation-for-exit package. The research below covers every dimension needed to build and price that offering.

---

## **Australia's succession crisis is accelerating faster than the market can absorb**

The numbers are stark. Of Australia's **2.73 million actively trading businesses**, roughly 22% — more than 500,000 — are owned by principals aged 60 or older. Among baby boomer SME owners (aged 60–78), **48% intend to exit within one to five years**, with 87% citing retirement as the primary motivation. A record 436,000 new businesses launched in 2024, and 370,500 exited, creating annual churn of over 800,000 businesses.

Yet the sell-side infrastructure is woefully inadequate. Only around **14,000–18,000 businesses are formally listed for sale at any time** — roughly 3.7% of annual exits. The remainder simply close, wind down, or dissolve. The widely cited statistic that **only 20–30% of listed businesses actually sell** appears to hold in Australia, meaning the vast majority of business value built over decades evaporates at retirement.

Professional services firms represent a particularly large and valuable segment. IBISWorld counts approximately **277,000 professional services businesses** in Australia, including 36,700 accounting firms, 17,500 financial planning practices, and nearly 95,000 management consulting firms. Combined sector revenue exceeds **$305 billion**. Specialist broker DMY reports that accounting practices are currently in a "seller's market" with record prices, averaging **72 buyer enquiries per metro listing** and 80% reaching Heads of Agreement within 90 days. Metro accounting practices sell at **$1.14 per dollar of revenue** — above the historical $1:$1 benchmark.

The demand side is strong. Millennials and Gen Z show high entrepreneurial intent — 75% of Gen Z respondents in MYOB's research are considering starting or buying a business. However, **younger buyers have non-negotiable expectations around digital readiness, documented systems, and operational independence** that most boomer-owned practices cannot meet. Only 9–19% of boomer businesses use cloud-based solutions for core functions. This mismatch between what's being sold and what buyers want creates the core opportunity for ThinkSwift.

A critical financing gap compounds the problem. Australian banks remain reluctant to lend against intangible assets like client relationships and recurring revenue, and Australia has no equivalent of the US Small Business Administration loan guarantee program. This means buyers prioritise businesses where they can quickly verify value and reduce risk — exactly the characteristics that systemisation delivers.

## **What transforms a professional services firm from "practice" to "turnkey asset"**

The single most important factor determining whether a professional services business sells — and at what price — is **owner dependency**. The International Business Brokers Association reports that owner-dependent businesses sell for **50–70% less** than systemised equivalents, if they sell at all. Australian valuers consistently identify "key person reliance" as the most significant factor for SME valuations.

A genuinely turnkey professional services business requires infrastructure across six domains:

**Operational documentation** forms the backbone. This includes a comprehensive operations manual (business playbook), standard operating procedures for every repeatable process, an organisational chart with clear accountability, role descriptions with KPIs, and documented meeting rhythms. Xero Australia advises sellers to "write down how the business operates, who's responsible for what, what order things get done in, and what systems you use — think of this as a manual for running the business."

**Client relationship institutionalisation** is where most professional services firms fail. Law firms experience client turnover of roughly **45% for top clients** even without partner departures. The solution requires formal engagement letters replacing handshake arrangements, client segmentation by revenue and risk, multiple staff members servicing each client, documented service level agreements, and a communications protocol for ownership transition. No single client should represent more than 10–15% of revenue.

**Financial transparency** demands three to five years of normalised, clean financial statements with owner add-backs clearly documented. Businesses run for "tax efficiency" (suppressed reported profits) appear less valuable to buyers. Future Maintainable Earnings calculations — the standard Australian valuation methodology — rely on provable, consistent financial performance.

**Technology infrastructure** must be cloud-based, transferable, and modern. The expected stack for a professional services firm includes practice management software (Karbon for accounting, LEAP or Clio for legal, Accelo or Productive for consulting), a CRM (HubSpot or Salesforce), cloud accounting (Xero or MYOB), document management (SharePoint or Google Workspace), and Australian-specific tools like Ignition for proposals, Employment Hero for HR, and Fathom for reporting.

**Management independence** means a second-in-command or general manager who can run day-to-day operations, cross-trained staff, delegated decision-making authority, and performance management systems that don't require the owner's involvement. The goal, as NAB Australia frames it: "Prepare your business as if you intend to franchise it."

**Compliance and risk documentation** covers professional registrations, insurance policies, privacy compliance, workplace health and safety, and — from 1 July 2026 — AML/CTF programs for accountants. All licences, memberships, and regulatory obligations must be current and documented.

## **The SOP architecture that underpins a saleable firm**

A comprehensive SOP library for a professional services firm spans approximately **60–80 discrete procedures** across seven categories. The critical ones, in order of impact on sale value, are:

The **client lifecycle** SOPs are highest priority: lead handling, client qualification, proposal preparation, engagement letter execution, onboarding (welcome pack, data collection, system setup, kickoff meeting), service delivery workflow by service type, quality assurance review, billing and collections, client feedback, complaint resolution, and offboarding. These demonstrate to buyers that client relationships are institutional, not personal.

**Service delivery** SOPs vary by firm type but must include project/matter initiation, resource scheduling, time recording, progress reporting, deliverable review and approval, document version control, quality control checklists, deadline management, and post-engagement reviews. These prove the firm can deliver consistent quality without the owner.

**Financial operations** SOPs cover pricing methodology, invoicing, collections, expense management, payroll, BAS/tax lodgement, bank reconciliation, and monthly/quarterly/annual reporting cadences. Clean financial operations directly increase buyer confidence and valuation multiples.

**HR and people** SOPs address recruitment, onboarding (first day through first 90 days), performance reviews, professional development, leave management, and exit procedures. Staff retention is critical — buyers need confidence the team will stay post-acquisition.

For building these SOPs, the most relevant Australian framework is **SYSTEMology**, developed by Melbourne entrepreneur David Jenyns. Its seven-step process (Define, Assign, Extract, Organise, Integrate, Scale, Optimise) and concept of the "Critical Client Flow" provides a practical methodology specifically designed for SME systemisation. Jenyns' key insight — that the business owner is the worst person to create the systems, and the work should be delegated to a "Systems Champion" using the firm's best team members — aligns well with ThinkSwift's proposed done-for-you model.

The **Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS)** from Gino Wickman's *Traction* provides the overarching business operating system structure: Vision, People, Data, Issues, Process, and Traction. Used by over 280,000 businesses globally, EOS establishes the meeting rhythms, accountability structures, and goal-setting frameworks that buyers expect. The two frameworks complement each other — SYSTEMology for the process of creating systems, EOS for the ongoing operating system.

For SOP hosting and management, the leading platforms are **Trainual** (from $249/month, best for structured onboarding), **SweetProcess** ($99/month for up to 20 users, best for clean SOP documentation), and **Process Street** (from $1,500/month, best for workflow automation). For budget-conscious firms, Notion provides flexible, affordable documentation. Video-based SOPs via Loom complement written procedures effectively.

## **The competitive landscape reveals a clear gap for ThinkSwift**

The Australian market for exit preparation services is fragmented across three disconnected provider types, and **no firm currently offers what ThinkSwift proposes**.

**Exit strategy advisors** focus on valuation, tax planning, and transaction execution. The market leader is **Succession Plus** (founded by Dr Craig West), Australia's largest exit planning firm with over 1,000 clients, a proprietary 21-step process, and the Capitaliz software platform. **Exit Advisory Group** (Sydney, led by Simon Bedard) won Business Consulting Company of the Year in 2024 and offers end-to-end M\&A advisory. **Venero Capital Advisors** provides sell-side M\&A with an exit readiness assessment across 20 key areas. These firms handle the strategic and transactional dimensions but **do not build operational systems or SOPs**.

**Management consultants and systemisation specialists** offer process documentation but rarely connect it to exit outcomes. Firms like Organising Works (Brisbane, Trainual Certified Partner) offer "SmartOps Pathway" packages. ISO 9001 consultants build quality management systems. But these providers **don't understand business sale dynamics or valuation drivers**.

**Business brokers** list and sell businesses but provide minimal preparation support. Specialist accounting practice brokers like DMY, Rob Knights & Co, and Practice4Sale focus on matching buyers and sellers rather than making businesses sale-ready.

The gap is clear and has been articulated by industry observers. Morgan & Westfield notes "a large divide between those who sell businesses and those who prepare businesses for sale — there is little crossover." No Australian firm combines deep operational systemisation with exit preparation as a single, integrated service. No firm offers AI-powered SOP development or process documentation. And critically, **almost no firm publishes transparent pricing** — the market operates entirely on "call for a quote," creating friction and uncertainty for time-poor business owners.

The technology platform **Bstar** deserves attention as a partial competitor. It provides accountants with a patented valuation algorithm and automated business advisory programs, enabling them to offer advisory services at $250–$10,000 per client engagement. While Bstar is a tool for accountants rather than a direct competitor to ThinkSwift, its model demonstrates the demand for productised, technology-enabled business advisory.

This serves as a low-risk entry point that leads to larger engagements and gives the owner a clear picture of what's needed.

Positioned for businesses actively preparing for sale in 12–24 months.

## **Australian legal and compliance requirements demand profession-specific expertise**

The legal landscape for selling professional services businesses varies significantly by profession type, and ThinkSwift's deliverables must account for these differences.

**Accounting firms** require sellers and buyers to hold Public Practice Certificates from CPA Australia or CA ANZ. Any structural changes must be notified to the professional body **at least 10 business days prior**. A critical new requirement: from **1 July 2026**, accountants must have AML/CTF programs in place under AUSTRAC regulations — practices being acquired or transferring clients face specific compliance procedures. The repeal of the accountants' AFSL exemption in 2016 means any firm providing financial advice must address AFSL coverage in the sale.

**Legal firms** face the most restrictive requirements. Buyers must hold a principal-level Australian Practising Certificate and complete a Practice Management Course. Trust account obligations must be carefully managed during transition. The Australian Solicitors' Conduct Rules impose strict confidentiality obligations — client files contain privileged material that cannot be disclosed to buyers during due diligence without client consent. State-by-state Law Society requirements add complexity.

**Financial planning firms** present a unique challenge: the AFSL is **non-transferable**. Buyers must either hold their own licence, be authorised representatives under another licence, or acquire the company holding the AFSL via share sale. ASIC must be notified of changes to responsible managers and authorised representatives. All advisers must meet FASEA education standards (approved degree or equivalent required since 1 January 2026).

**Cross-profession requirements** include Privacy Act compliance (asset sales involving personal information constitute "trading in personal information" and trigger full Privacy Act coverage), Fair Work Act transfer-of-business provisions (employees who transfer within three months must have prior service recognised for personal/carer's leave and parental leave), and the small business CGT concessions. The **four CGT concessions** are particularly valuable for retiring owners: the 15-year exemption (entire gain disregarded if owned 15+ years and retiring at 55+), the 50% active asset reduction, the retirement exemption (up to $500,000 lifetime), and the small business rollover. Eligibility requires aggregated turnover under $2 million or net assets under $6 million.

For ThinkSwift's compliance documentation deliverable, a pre-sale checklist must cover: current business registrations (ABN, ACN, business name), professional licences and memberships, tax compliance (income tax, GST/BAS, PAYG, FBT, super guarantee), employment records, insurance policies, privacy policies, client contracts reviewed for assignment restrictions, and — for relevant professions — trust account reconciliation and AML/CTF programs. Restraint of trade clauses in sale contracts are viewed favourably by courts (more so than employment restraints) and **two-to-three-year non-compete periods** are standard.

## **The ROI argument that sells the service**

The value proposition for ThinkSwift's offering rests on hard numbers. The evidence is consistent across multiple sources: **systemisation routinely delivers 5–20x returns on exit preparation investment** through higher multiples, faster sales, more cash at closing, and dramatically improved probability of a successful transaction.

The valuation gap between owner-dependent and systemised businesses is substantial across every professional services category:

| Firm type | Owner-dependent multiple | Systemised multiple | Revenue/earnings basis |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Accounting | $0.60–$0.80 per $ revenue | $1.00–$1.20+ per $ revenue | Recurring fees |
| Accounting (larger) | 2.5x EBIT | 3.5–4.2x EBIT | Future Maintainable Earnings |
| Legal | 0.5x gross revenue | 0.8–1.0x+ gross revenue | Gross fees |
| Financial planning | 2.0x recurring revenue | 3.0–3.5x recurring revenue | Recurring fees |
| Marketing agency | 3x EBITDA | 5–6x EBITDA | Adjusted EBITDA |
| Management consulting | 3x EBITDA | 5–8x EBITDA | Adjusted EBITDA |

Consider a concrete scenario. An accounting firm generating $1.5 million in revenue with $300,000 adjusted EBIT, currently highly owner-dependent, might sell for **$750,000** (2.5x EBIT). After 12–18 months of systemisation costing $60,000–$80,000, the same firm with documented SOPs, a capable management team, institutionalised client relationships, and clean financials could command **$1.2 million** (4x EBIT). The **net gain of $370,000–$390,000 represents a 5–6.5x return** on the preparation investment.

For a financial planning practice with $2 million in recurring revenue, moving from 2.0x to 3.0x recurring revenue through systemisation represents **$2 million in additional enterprise value** against a preparation cost of $80,000–$120,000 — a **17–25x return**.

Beyond the multiple uplift, systemisation delivers three additional value benefits. First, it **dramatically increases the probability of sale**. With 70% of listed businesses failing to find a buyer, and preparation being the primary differentiator between successful and failed sales, the shift from "probably won't sell" to "probably will sell" is itself worth the investment. Second, it **reduces time to sale** from 12–24+ months to 3–9 months, meaning the owner recovers the preparation investment faster and reduces ongoing risk. Third, it **reduces earn-out and clawback exposure** — buyers of well-documented businesses are more willing to pay cash at closing rather than requiring the seller to remain involved through earn-out structures.

The Exit Planning Institute's finding that **planned exits yield 16x more than unplanned ones** provides the ultimate headline statistic. For a boomer owner whose business represents their retirement nest egg — and 34% of retiring boomers expect exactly this — the question isn't whether they can afford exit preparation, but whether they can afford to skip it.

The addressable market for professional services exit preparation alone is estimated at **$33–$110 million annually** over the next five years, based on approximately 55,000 professional services firms in transition and average advisory spend of $15,000–$50,000 per engagement. This excludes the broader SME market.

Third, **specialise by profession** — the compliance, licensing, and client-transfer requirements differ so materially between accounting, legal, and financial planning firms that a generic offering will underperform a verticalised one.

The Australian SYSTEMology framework, developed locally by David Jenyns, provides a natural methodological foundation that resonates with Australian business owners and aligns with ThinkSwift's proposed approach. Combined with AI-powered SOP generation, process documentation, and knowledge extraction, ThinkSwift can deliver in weeks what traditional consultants deliver in months — at price points that make the ROI argument irresistible.
